Terpedia Protein Docking

A provenance-aware platform for terpene-target discovery

Terpedia

19 July 2026

Executive Summary

Terpedia is building a scientific study platform for asking which proteins a terpene may interact with, why that hypothesis is plausible, and what evidence would change our mind. Protein docking is one component of that platform. It is useful for ranking hypotheses and designing experiments; it is not, by itself, a measurement of binding.

Our position is deliberately practical:

  • Prepare broadly and cheaply. Normalize structures, generate reusable ligand inputs, curate protein structures, define pockets, and retain all artifacts with checksums.
  • Validate before ranking. A receptor is not eligible for candidate docking merely because a PDBQT file can be produced. Co-crystal redocking, chemical-template checks, pocket plausibility, and experimental anchors gate downstream use.
  • Spend selectively. Use inexpensive preparation and filters across large inventories, then apply GNINA/Vina or higher-quality methods to experimentally grounded target panels and prioritized hypotheses.
  • Report uncertainty as a result. Blocked preparation, unstable redocking, method disagreement, and missing evidence remain visible in the knowledge graph.

This makes Terpedia complementary to experimental pharmacology and existing docking software. Our differentiator is not a single score. It is the connection between chemical identity, biological context, structure preparation, computation, provenance, and reproducible research outputs.

1. The Problem

Terpene pharmacology has an unusually wide hypothesis space. A single compound can appear in plant chemistry, natural-product databases, target-fishing predictions, assay records, literature, and product formulations under different names and stereochemical descriptions. Human proteins are similarly heterogeneous: some have high-quality co-crystal structures, some have multiple conformations, some have missing residues or cofactors, and many have no experimentally resolved structure suitable for a particular question.

Naive docking pipelines hide this complexity. They often:

  1. resolve names without preserving the source identity;
  2. silently choose a protonation, tautomer, stereoisomer, or salt form;
  3. remove cofactors or difficult residues until preparation succeeds;
  4. dock into an arbitrary or oversized box;
  5. report a favorable score without receptor validation;
  6. treat a computational rank as experimental affinity.

Those shortcuts create polished but weak evidence. Terpedia is positioned around the opposite discipline: every computational result should be answerable in terms of the exact molecule, protein structure, pocket, software, parameters, source, artifact, and validation state that produced it.

2. Our Positioning

2.1 Terpedia is an evidence-to-hypothesis system

Terpedia connects four evidence layers:

Layer Question Typical output
Knowledge What is known about this terpene, target, pathway, or assay? Provenance-linked claims and references
Structure Can the chemical and protein inputs be represented responsibly? Canonical identities, prepared structures, pockets, blockers
Computation Given those inputs, which hypotheses rank highest? Docking scores, poses, consensus ranks, uncertainty
Experiment What observation would validate or refute the hypothesis? Assay candidates, controls, measured interactions

Docking is therefore downstream of knowledge and structure, and upstream of experiment. A docking result that lacks its input history is not a reusable scientific asset.

2.2 Terpedia is not a binding oracle

The platform should use language such as:

  • “computationally prioritized for testing”;
  • “compatible with the registered pocket under this protocol”;
  • “rank-consistent across these scoring functions”;
  • “unsupported because receptor preparation failed”; and
  • “requires experimental validation.”

It should avoid presenting docking as proof of affinity, efficacy, selectivity, mechanism, or clinical benefit. A high score can reflect pocket geometry, receptor state, charge assumptions, scoring-function bias, or an artifact of preparation.

2.3 Terpedia is a study platform, not only an endpoint

Every research question should be able to produce a versioned study repository containing:

  • a frozen question and protocol;
  • a Colab notebook or equivalent reproducible execution;
  • structured query responses and serialized data;
  • input and output manifests;
  • source and artifact checksums;
  • figures and tables;
  • an editable paper, report, DOCX/PDF, and XLSX export;
  • review notes, failures, and unresolved questions.

This is the product surface that turns a chat question into inspectable science.

3. Technical Architecture

  A[Terpedia KB and source records] --> B[Identity and provenance layer]

  D -->|blocked| E[Retained failure and review queue]
  D -->|eligible| F[Cheap scoring and target filters]
  F --> G[GNINA/Vina focused docking]

3.1 Identity registry

Ligands are keyed by chemically meaningful identifiers rather than display names. The registry records canonical and isomeric SMILES, InChI, InChIKey, formula, stereocenters, source identifiers, aliases, and identity policy. A change in stereochemistry, charge, tautomer, or source structure creates a new revision rather than overwriting an old one.

The current preparation work has been extended to stage 177,449 structure-bearing records from the Terport COCONUT-PubChem export as 178 deterministic compressed chunks. This is an input inventory, not a claim that every record is a simple volatile terpene or immediately suitable for docking. Chemical class, size, charge, structural alerts, and biological context must remain available for filtering.

3.2 Protein and pocket registry

For each protein structure, Terpedia records:

  • UniProt and organism mapping;
  • PDB identifier, assembly, chain, and structure quality;
  • missing residues and unresolved atoms;
  • co-crystallized ligands and cofactors;
  • protonation and hydrogen policy;
  • binding-site coordinates and derivation method;
  • preparation software and version;
  • artifact URI and SHA-256;
  • redocking results and eligibility state.

The registry distinguishes source_ready, prepared_unvalidated, ready, blocked, failed, and superseded. A ready state requires a persisted artifact and a successful registered validation gate. A technical failure is never converted into a favorable result by silently deleting chemistry.

3.3 Evidence and provenance graph

The knowledge base should answer not only “what is the score?” but also:

  • Which exact structure was docked?
  • Which source supplied it?
  • Which preparation revision generated the PDBQT?
  • Which pocket and cofactors were used?
  • Which software, seed, and scoring function produced the pose?
  • Was the receptor redocked successfully?
  • Are there experimental interaction records for this terpene-target pair?
  • What alternative methods disagree?

Firestore holds queryable registry metadata, while large PDB, PDBQT, conformer, pose, notebook, and report artifacts live in immutable, checksummed object storage. This separation supports both API access and reproducible repository exports.

4. Computation Strategy

4.1 Broad preparation, narrow docking

Large-scale preparation is comparatively inexpensive and reusable. It can include identity normalization, descriptor calculation, conformer generation, ligand PDBQT preparation, protein-source registration, and pocket metadata. The output is a library of inspectable inputs, not a declaration that all pairs should be docked.

Docking should then be staged:

  1. Eligibility filters: chemical validity, stereochemistry, charge policy, protein structure quality, pocket plausibility, and biological relevance.
  2. Cheap triage: target-fishing evidence, similarity to known ligands, experimental interaction data, pharmacophore compatibility, and fast docking or rescoring.
  3. Focused GNINA/Vina screen: a measured benchmark on a curated target panel, preferably in rescore mode before expensive refinement.
  4. High-quality follow-up: receptor ensembles, flexible side chains, explicit waters, molecular dynamics, free-energy methods, or external commercial software where the hypothesis justifies the cost.
  5. Experimental handoff: prioritize assays with controls, orthogonal readouts, and a clear falsification criterion.

4.2 Validation gates

The current Terpedia reproduction uses symmetry-aware co-crystal redocking as a practical gate. A registered receptor becomes eligible only when the predefined RMSD criterion passes across repeated seeds. This does not prove that all prospective ligands will dock correctly; it establishes a minimum test that the pocket and preparation can recover a known pose under the selected protocol.

The gate should be supplemented by:

  • alternate co-crystal ligands where available;
  • receptor-ensemble agreement;
  • pocket-volume and ligandability checks;
  • sensitivity to protonation and tautomer policy;
  • cross-engine rank agreement;
  • known positive and negative controls;
  • comparison with experimentally measured terpene-protein interactions.

Failures are first-class outputs. They identify where manual curation, a better structure, a cofactor template, or an experiment is needed.

4.3 GNINA’s role

GNINA is a useful open-source workhorse for focused screening because it combines classical docking with learned CNN scoring and can run in low-compute or higher-quality modes. Terpedia should use it as one method in a method ensemble, not as an authority. Default use should begin with measured CPU/GPU throughput and rescore; refinement and CNN ensembles should be reserved for a smaller set of hypotheses.

Where a reference paper uses MOE or another proprietary workflow, Terpedia should describe the result as a cross-engine recapitulation unless the same engine, release, parameters, and inputs are available. Numerical scores from different scoring functions should not be averaged as though they share a scale. Rank and enrichment comparisons are more defensible.

5. Cost and Scale Position

The economic advantage is architectural:

Work Cost posture Why
Identity, descriptors, and ligand preparation Mostly local or scale-to-zero CPU Run once; reuse across all targets
Pocket curation and receptor preparation Bounded CPU plus scientific review Spend where structures are plausible
100-system benchmark Small GPU/CPU experiment Measure actual throughput and enrichment
2,000-target focused panel Controlled GPU campaign Expand only when validation supports it
Whole-proteome screen Last-stage hypothesis generation Requires prepared pockets and strong filters

The active cost control is not simply a dollar ceiling. It is a scientific budget: a failed receptor should stop downstream docking, and a method that does not enrich known positives should not be scaled merely because it is cheap. The planned sub-$100 preparation campaign is therefore designed to produce reusable ligand artifacts and receipts under a hard budget, while protein preparation and docking remain gated separately.

6. Competitive and Scientific Differentiation

Terpedia’s differentiation is the combination of:

  1. Domain focus: terpene, cannabis, plant, product, metabolic, and regulatory context in one knowledge environment.
  2. Symbolic and statistical reasoning: graph queries, structured claims, semantic relationships, embeddings, target-fishing models, and molecular computation are complementary rather than interchangeable.
  3. Provenance by default: every answer can expose its sources, transformations, artifacts, and uncertainty.
  4. Agentic study orchestration: LangGraph coordinates researchers, chemists, target-fishing methods, docking tools, reviewers, and an Editor agent while keeping tool outputs authoritative.
  5. Reproducible publication: the same study can produce a chat answer, API response, Colab notebook, paper, figures, and data tables.
  6. Failure-aware science: blocked targets and inconclusive results are preserved instead of being hidden by a language model or a pipeline retry.

The result is closer to a scientific operating layer for terpene research than to a generic docking web service.

7. Product Experience

Users should be able to ask:

Which human proteins are plausible targets for beta-caryophyllene, and what evidence supports each hypothesis?

Terpedia should answer with a compact evidence table containing:

  • the target and biological context;
  • experimental interactions, if any;
  • target-fishing support;
  • structure and pocket quality;
  • preparation and redocking state;
  • docking method and rank;
  • disagreements and uncertainty;
  • links to the underlying notebook, artifacts, and references;
  • a proposed validation experiment.

The chat UI is the conversational surface. The API and Colab notebook are equally important surfaces for article review, batch analysis, and research projects. An Editor agent should turn the same evidence bundle into a methods-aware whitepaper or paper without inventing data.

8. Roadmap

Near term

  • Complete the bounded, chunked ligand-preparation campaign and publish its manifest.
  • Add structure-quality and pocket-ligandability filters to the registry.
  • Build a validated experimental terpene-protein interaction set for enrichment testing.
  • Benchmark GNINA rescore against Vina, Vinardo, and the validated controls.
  • Add API queries for serialized Python data and Colab study initialization.

Medium term

  • Curate a human protein target panel with structure, pocket, expression, and disease-context evidence.
  • Add receptor ensembles and alternate protonation/cofactor policies as explicit revisions.
  • Implement active-learning prioritization from experimental outcomes.
  • Compare selected targets against higher-quality docking and molecular-dynamics workflows.
  • Generate study-specific GitHub repositories with notebooks, artifacts, reports, and machine-readable provenance.

Long term

  • Screen a filtered human proteome for a defined set of terpene hypotheses.
  • Integrate measured affinities, functional assays, metabolism, and exposure into a Bayesian evidence model.
  • Make every computational claim updateable when sources, structures, software, or experiments change.

9. Claims We Can Make

Terpedia can responsibly claim that it:

  • preserves exact chemical and structural inputs;
  • produces reproducible, checksummed preparation artifacts;
  • validates selected receptors against registered structural tests;
  • compares computational methods and reports disagreement;
  • prioritizes hypotheses for experimental testing;
  • generates reproducible notebooks and research artifacts.

Terpedia cannot responsibly claim, from docking alone, that a terpene binds a protein in vivo, produces a therapeutic effect, is selective, is safe, or works in a product or patient. Those claims require appropriate biochemical, cellular, pharmacokinetic, toxicological, and clinical evidence.

10. Conclusion

Terpedia’s protein-docking position is disciplined ambition. We want to evaluate terpene hypotheses at useful scale, including against large human-protein panels, but we will not confuse scale with validity. Cheap preparation, persistent provenance, strict receptor gates, measured model comparison, and reproducible study outputs let the platform grow without losing scientific meaning.

The core promise is simple: Terpedia should help a researcher move from “could this terpene interact with this protein?” to “here is the evidence, here is the computation, here are the uncertainties, and here is the next experiment.”
